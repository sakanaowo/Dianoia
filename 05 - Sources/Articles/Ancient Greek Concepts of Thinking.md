## Main idea

Ancient Greek has multiple terms related to thinking, understanding, mind, and practical wisdom.  
The important distinction is that these words do not all mean "thinking" in the same way.

## Key terms

- [[φρονέω - phroneō]]: practical, action-guiding thinking.
- [[φρόνησις - phronēsis]]: practical wisdom or prudence.
- [[φρόνημα - phronēma]]: mindset, disposition, settled attitude.
- [[νοέω - noeō]]: intellectual perception or comprehension.
- [[νόησις - noēsis]]: act of understanding or intellection.
- [[νοῦς - nous]]: intellect, rational mind.
- [[φρήν - phrēn]]: embodied seat of thought, emotion, and intention.

## Summary in my words

The text contrasts two families of Greek terms:

- The **φρον-** family is closer to practical judgment, attitude, and action.
- The **νο-** family is closer to intellectual perception, understanding, and rational thought.
- [[νοῦς - nous]] is more abstract and rational.
- [[φρήν - phrēn]] is more embodied and connected to emotion, desire, and moral orientation.

## Critical notes

This explanation is useful as a beginner-level map, but it may oversimplify the historical usage of these terms.

Possible weak points:

- [[φρήν - phrēn]] is not simply “mindset”; in older Greek it can refer to the midriff, heart, inward faculty, emotion, or seat of thought.
- [[φρόνησις - phronēsis]] becomes especially important in Aristotle, where it means practical wisdom, not merely “common sense.”
- [[νοῦς - nous]] can mean intellect, mind, insight, or divine/intelligible intellect depending on author and period.
- The meaning changes across Homer, Plato, Aristotle, Stoicism, and later Christian philosophy.
